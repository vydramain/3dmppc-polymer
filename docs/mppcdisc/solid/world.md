# World, Setting & Tone

## Setting

An alternate **post-Soviet Russia, 1990s**. A grey industrial town of
Khrushchyovka apartment blocks, cracked sidewalks, courtyards, and a factory
that never really closes. Everything is worn, utilitarian, and slightly too
quiet. Beneath the mundane surface, **Alkoldun folklore** leaks in: rituals,
folk-horror spirits, and objects that behave as if they remember something.

## Tone

Satirical folk horror. Two registers held at once:

- **Mundane oppression** — the exhausting sameness of the daily grind, chronic
  fatigue, bleak weather, fluorescent light.
- **Uncanny dread** — the routine mutates. Familiar blocks rearrange; folklore
  caricatures walk the streets; the factory's "productivity" turns occult.

The humor is deadpan and empathetic, never mocking the protagonist. The horror
is atmospheric and creeping, not gore-driven. As the closing note of the
original design put it: *make the mundane oppressive and the absurd familiar.*

## The day loop as fiction

The **Home → Street → Factory → Home** cycle is the narrative spine, not just a
level structure. Each repetition is the same day worn a little thinner. Small
authored mutations (a new poster, a flickering light, a rearranged room, a
stranger on the street) accumulate to signal the protagonist's fraying psyche
and the world's slow corruption. Progress is measured less in "levels cleared"
than in **how strange the day has become**.

## Places

- **Home** — a single cramped Khrushchyovka apartment room. A radio/TV that
  flips a mood flag; bleak courtyard views through the window. The one refuge,
  and the one place that visibly mutates between loops.
- **The Street** — a straight, modular stretch of the town: sidewalks, apartment
  facades, fences, kiosks, alleys. Recognisable yet subtly wrong; alleys hide
  encounters and stolen ritual parts. Ends at a checkpoint gate to the factory.
- **The Factory** — a hangar-like industrial hall: lamp-assembly tables, a
  welding station, a finishing conveyor, and a central open floor reserved for
  the day's climactic encounter. Labour and ritual are the same act here.

## Motifs

- **The lamppost / pillar.** Assembling a lamppost is the factory's ritual and
  the game's recurring object of power (see [`mechanics.md`](mechanics.md)).
- **Folklore as bureaucracy.** The bosses are folk-tale figures wearing the
  masks of 90s institutions — the warehouse, accounting, the oligarchy (see
  [`characters.md`](characters.md)).
- **Improvised survival.** No heroic arsenal; only what's lying around — bricks,
  pipes, and the parts you build.

## Fiction guardrails

- Keep the world grounded in **authentic 1990s Russian industrial** texture; no
  modern or Western fashion, no bright folklore ornament pasted on.
- Folklore enters through **behaviour and dread**, not decoration.
- Satire stays character-empathetic; the target is the system, not the worker.
